ESE Dual-Degree Student John Emmons Awarded NSF Fellowship

4/11/2016

John Emmons (BSEE, BSCOE, MSCS 2016) has been awarded the National Science Foundation Graduate Student Fellowship. The National Science Foundation Graduate Research Fellowship Program (NSF-GRFP) is an annual grant awarded by the National Science Foundation to approximately 2,000 doctoral-level students in the natural, social, and engineering sciences at US institutions. John is one of seven computer systems students in the country to be awarded the fellowship this year.

"The NSF Fellowship is one of the most prestigious awards given to young scientists, mathematicians, and engineers--being selected is an incredible honor and will hopefully mark the beginning of many exciting academic opportunities to come," John says. "My success was in large part a result of an extraordinary amount work by many faculty members, but especially Professors Jeremy Buhler (Washington University), Oscar Bruno (Caltech), Onur Mutlu (Carnegie Mellon), and Tim Urness (Drake University). If it were not for these professors' support and the resources for undergraduate research at Drake and Wash U., I could not have attained this distinction."

The title of John's NSF research proposal was "Application Development Paradigms for Emerging, Hybrid CPU/FPGA, Many-Core Processor Architectures." He says, "I chose this topic because I am less interested in the guts of any particular application domain, such as machine learning or computational physics, than I am in developing a principled approach to solving these problems on next-generation computer systems. Thousand core CPUs with on-chip GPU and FPGA accelerators are rapidly becoming a reality due to the power wall; coming up with programming abstractions that enable application developers to write high performance code on these complex devices will be vital to continued advancement in nearly all industries and fields of science."

Bio: John Emmons began his undergraduate education at Drake University where he triple majored in computer science, mathematics, and physics. After completing all degree requirements in three years, he chose to postpone graduation and to transfer to Washington University through the 3-2 Engineering Dual Degree program to further his studies in the computational sciences. While at Washington University, he has completed two BS degrees in computer and electrical engineering respectively and an MS in computer science. In the end, he will receive degrees from both Drake and Washington University.

John has also distinguished himself by publishing research in top journals such as Oxford Bioinformatics and Physical Review A and by winning several notable awards including the Barry Goldwater Scholarship as a sophomore and full-tuition scholarships at both Drake (Drake Physics Prize) and Washington University.

John recently accepted an offer to start his PhD in computer science this fall at Stanford University.
