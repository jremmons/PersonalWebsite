Reflections on Our Study Abroad Program in Germany, 2009

During the week of May 11th to May 16th, 2009, six undergraduate students from the Electrical and Systems Engineering department (ESE) at Washington University in Saint Louis (WUSTL), participated in a study abroad program at Germany. The students studied medical imaging methods at the MEG-Center at the University of Tuebingen (UT), and the Max Planck Institute for Biological Cybernetics, Germany. They were accompanied by our Ph.D. student Patricio S. La Rosa.  This program was organized by Dr. Arye Nehorai, Chair and Professor of ESE, WUSTL, and Dr. Hubert Preissl, Assistant Professor at MEG Center, UT. The schedule of the program can be found at:

http://ese.wustl.edu/undergraduateprograms/Pages/ESEStudyAbroad2009Program.aspx

The following are write ups of reflections written by the students about their experience.


Our trip opened my eyes to a field [Neuroscience and Neuroimaging] which I had previously not seriously considered as a potential avenue for research or even graduate studies. I am entering my senior year at WashU (majoring in Electrical Engineering and Computer Engineering), and right now I am trying to figure out my grad school plans. I am fairly overwhelmed with the many possibilities I face, but experiences like this give me a much more comfortable picture of a potential pathway.

This program made me realize what an amazing field neuroscience is and that as an EE, it IS completely possible to enter such a field. While in Tübingen, I really enjoyed meeting the researchers and seeing their work in progress. This extension of my classroom experiences was very valuable in forming a more accurate perspective on medical research with respect to an engineering background. Several of us students who attended are planning on continuing this research at least into the next school year.

Although, not the focus of the program, this also enabled us to experience a completely new culture(s) and travel to places that we had never been. Personally, I enjoyed this just as much as the educational experience. Due to time and money constraints, I had no previous plans of visiting Europe for many years to come, and I'm so glad that changed. I spent several extra days traveling Germany (which I'm absolutely in love with now) and spent a week in Spain afterwards.

Michael J. Steinbock BSEE 2010

Not only did the Introduction to Multimodal Imaging Program educate me about medical imaging technologies, but it also encouraged me to maintain an open mind about my future as a Systems Engineer. Although Systems Engineering has a wide variety of applications, I never really thought about its place in the medical world. Our hosts at the University of Tübingen gave me a new perspective. Advances in medical technology require the collaboration of several disciplines, and a Systems Engineer’s analytical and mathematical skills are integral to the design process. This experience encouraged me to investigate other applications of Systems Engineering and become more knowledgeable about my field.

Going to Germany further enhanced my experience. Tübingen has some of the most advanced imaging technology, but more importantly, its community has a very positive attitude towards research. Our hosts were truly excited about their research and jumped at the opportunity to share their knowledge about Neuroscience and Neuroimaging. Their enthusiasm for Neuroscience and Neuroimaging research transferred to the group and, as far as I can tell, has compelled many students to pursue research this coming school year.

It really speaks volumes about Washington University’s commitment to the educational experience that six undergraduates had this opportunity. I was very grateful to be part of this trip, and I hope that future students will have the chance to further their education through a similar experience.

Jessica Stigile BSSSE 2010

When I first arrived in Germany, I did not know what to expect. I did not speak a word of German, I had no background in medical imaging technology, and I didn't even know anyone who was going to be there with me. To tell the truth I was a little worried about the trip.

However, as soon as the program started, I realized that this was going to be a great experience. The first day, we were given personal lectures by amazing professors on anything and everything related to MEG's and fMEG's. We toured the world class MEG facility which we were lucky enough to stay at for an entire week. And that was just the first day.

One afternoon that particularly stood out to me was our visit to the pre-clinical site. This facility was kind of a halfway house for technology, where scientists and engineers took the latest in medical imaging knowledge and used it to create devices that could then be commercialized at a company like Siemens or Philips. We saw the world's first MRI/PET scanner, which allowed cancer researchers to study the affects of radiation on mice (with the PET) while still being able to tell where in the body the radiation was (with the MRI). Although that particular device was literally put together with duct tape and a long way off from being commercially available, the fact that it is the first MRI/PET scanner in the world was very cool.

Throughout the week, we visited the neuroscience graduate school, the dental school, the hospital, and the Max Planck Institute. At each location, we were shown around by world class researchers who were always extremely courteous and helpful. I never imagined having such personal interactions with such amazing people.

The city of Tübingen was also a delight. We spent many nights exploring the town and enjoying all the food, sights, and people. Tubingen is a quaint but beautiful city, and I always felt safe and comfortable there.

Going to Germany was a wonderful experience. We were introduced to amazing, cutting-edge technology. We were guided around by brilliant researchers who were nonetheless very kind and welcoming to some random undergraduates from America. And the place itself was just simply beautiful. I had a great time, and I cannot thank enough everyone who helped make the trip possible.

Ian Beil BSEE 2011

With everyone recalling their experiences in Tübingen, I imagine you have heard all about our daily activities, what we saw, and what we learned, so I won't repeat any of it here. I was absolutely amazed by the research that was going on and the brilliant minds driving the success. To sum it up, I will say that all together we took nearly 1000 photos between just four students with 2.3 gigabytes of them coming from my camera alone. The exposure was overwhelming (camera pun!).

I can't say for sure whether I will become a neuroscientist now, but in no way was the experience wasted on me because of that. This trip meant much more to me than just a taste of a fast-growing field. It makes all my hard work feel worthwhile and noticed. Rewards like this help motivate not just me, but any of the students that have had the good fortune to experience the enormous power of a Washington University in St. Louis education. I am now, more than ever, eager to give back to my department and school, and am wasting no time in doing so as IEEE President for next year. This trip will greatly help shape the events IEEE hosts in the future as a student organization whose objective it is to expand students’ minds and open their horizons to a world of possibilities.

That being said, I am, in fact, German and would not complain at the thought of living in Tübingen where we were overwhelmingly welcomed by other students, researchers, and the residents. So, studying and researching there is definitely a consideration and would be a great way to continue this fantastic University relationship.

All this in just five days.

Jeffrey Feiereisen BSEE 2010

My name is Jennifer Sisto and I was one of the Wash U students selected to participate in the program for an Introduction to Multimodal Imagining in Tubingen, Germany. I am a rising senior with an applied science major in Systems Science & Mathematics, with a second major in the business school in Healthcare Management.

As a scholarship recipient from the School of Engineering & Applied Science, I have benefited first-hand from contributions to the school since coming to Wash U. Although these gracious donations enabled me to come to Wash U and further my education, the contribution dedicated toward study abroad was something much different. It allowed our group of students to form an international connection with some of the top neuroscience researchers in the world. It showed us what opportunities are available for engineers outside of some more typical applications. It influenced us to think about bringing exciting and groundbreaking research projects back to Wash U so that we can further and deepen the lessons we learned in Tubingen.

Personally, I have benefited from this program in enormous ways. Although I was on a business track and looking to leave engineering, the research we were exposed to has reinspired me to pursue further schooling. As cheesy as it may sound, this trip has actually changed my life! Although I've been very active in the school through student organizations and leadership positions, I have never done a research project, and am now looking to begin one. After speaking with the other students throughout the week, I think that the experience left a similar impact on them.

Besides the positive educational and professional outcomes of this trip, I also (of course!) enjoyed seeing Germany. I actually was lucky enough to go as a high school student for 6 weeks in the summer after studying German for 5 years, but after that I have not been able to return. It was so much fun to practice my German language skills again. After being finished in Tubingen I was able to visit family friends in Aachen and Krefeld (outside of Dusseldorf), and fly back into Washington D.C. to visit my sister.

Overall this trip will resonate with me as one of the most influential times in my personal development; I was introduced to ground-breaking research, inspired by the goals and aspirations of my fellow classmates, and lastly influenced in my own personal journey in higher education.

Jennifer Sisto BSSE 2010

The Introduction to Multimodal Imaging program was very inspiring and eye-opening as it showed us how engineering applications are used in the medical world. Choosing Systems Engineering as my major, I was aware of its applications in many different fields but I had never explored one to such an extent. It was very beneficial to have this discovery at a very important imaging center in the world (Tubingen) in a packed one week program.

We got the chance to listen to many scientists about their research in the imaging field, the technologies they were using, the problems they were facing and areas they wanted to invest more time in. Our program included lectures and tours given by scientists from many fields, such as biologists, physicists, chemists, mathematicians, electrical, systems and computer engineers, psychologists, psychiatrists, and medical doctors, providing us the opportunity to experience the interdisciplinary nature of research. I am majoring in Systems Engineering with a concentration in Economics and minor in Psychology, which is why I appreciated this interdisciplinary approach and found it especially significant.

We learned about not only one but many imaging modalities (MRIs, fMRIs, MEGs, EEGs, fMEGs, PET scans, and TMS) which gave us an extensive insight into the technologies in use. Some of the modalities, such as fetal MEG are very new areas of research; it was interesting to hear about how they were progressing. We addressed interesting questions such as how to combine certain modalities for better scientific results. Our discussions included both practical and scientific problems. We would have a lecture given by a psychologist on the Brain-Computer Interface and how engineers could advance behavioral ADHD therapy by developing better devices, followed by a lecture on SQUIDs sensors, the physics and optimization behind all these imaging machines.

The program was also very interactive and it incorporated visits to important labs in Tubingen. We visited the Max Planck Institute for Biological Cybernetics and observed ongoing projects in a professional research setting. We also visited the Laboratory for Imaging of the Werner Siemens Foundation, which gave us insight about an industrially funded and supported research setting. It was very exciting to learn about these research projects from the scientists conducting them while also being able to see the equipment and labs they use.

As a rising junior, I found the study abroad program in Germany very helpful as it made me think about my academic plans under a different light. I found multimodal imaging to be intriguing and I now definitely consider it as a field I can concentrate on after college. I am planning on taking the SSE elective on multimodal imaging to expand my knowledge on this area of study. In addition, it was motivating to talk about the possibility of bringing the MEG and EEG technologies to Washington University and creating opportunities for further studies on campus.

I found the chance to interact with fellow engineering students and visit a new country very exciting and enjoyable. Talking to other engineers on the program about academic interests and getting to know them better added a lot to my experience. I was more than happy to discover more about the German culture and history. I really enjoyed our stay in Tubingen and I consider Germany as a possible location for my studies after college.

I am very thankful for being given the chance to be a part of this academically and socially fulfilling program. I find the numerous opportunities available to us and the continuous support given to undergraduate students by our university and the SSE department significantly remarkable.

Zeynep Esin BSSSE 2011
